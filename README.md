## Policy Question

> **Are fare gates on the MBTA worth it?**

Do the revenues fare gates protect (by deterring evasion) exceed their total costs — capital, maintenance, and the ridership they suppress through friction? And what behavioral mechanisms drive fare evasion in the first place?

---

## Behavioral Economics Framing

Fare gates are fundamentally a **friction-based compliance mechanism**. Key concepts:

| Concept | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| **Friction / transaction costs** | Gates raise the cost of evasion but also the cost of legitimate boarding |
| **Default effects** | Gated systems make paying the *default*; honor systems make paying opt-in |
| **Present bias** | Evaders discount the low-probability risk of being caught |
| **Social norm cascades** | Visible evasion normalizes further evasion |
| **Loss aversion** | Fare payments are a salient, immediate "loss" — makes fare-free systems behaviorally attractive |
